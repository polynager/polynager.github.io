Short-term Production Function

Short-term Production Function

In the short run, firms face a situation where at least one input is fixed. In this example, capital (\( K^* \)) is fixed at 3 units, meaning the firm cannot adjust its level of capital in response to changes in labor or other conditions. This reflects a common reality in production, where certain inputs—such as machinery or space—cannot be quickly changed.

The production function is:

\[ Q = f(K^*, L) = 15 \cdot (K^*)^{0.25} \cdot L^{0.75} \]

Where:

  • Q: total output
  • K*: fixed capital in the short run, here \( K^* = 3 \)
  • L: labor input
  • Exponents 0.25 and 0.75: output elasticities of capital and labor
  • Coefficient 15: a scale factor representing productivity

Diminishing Marginal Returns

In the short run, as more labor \( L \) is added while keeping capital fixed, output rises but at a decreasing rate. This is the principle of diminishing marginal returns.

The marginal product of labor (MPL) is:

\[ MPL = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial L} = 15 \cdot (K^*)^{0.25} \cdot 0.75 \cdot L^{-0.25} \]

Since \( K^* \) is fixed, the MPL declines as more labor is employed, illustrating diminishing returns. In the long run, when capital can also vary, these constraints are reduced.

Short-Term Production Function and Marginal Product of Labor

Relationship Between MPL and APL in the Short-run

Relationship Between Marginal Product of Labor (MPL) and Average Product of Labor (APL)

In the short run, the relationship between the Marginal Product of Labor (MPL) and the Average Product of Labor (APL) is crucial for understanding how labor inputs contribute to production.

Marginal Product of Labor (MPL)

The Marginal Product of Labor measures the additional output produced by one more unit of labor:

\[ MPL = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial L} \]

It shows how much extra output is generated by hiring one more worker, holding capital fixed.

Average Product of Labor (APL)

The Average Product of Labor measures the average output produced per unit of labor:

\[ APL = \frac{Q}{L} \]

It reflects the productivity of labor on average, found by dividing total output by the number of workers.

Key Relationship

  • If \( MPL > APL \), then APL is rising.
  • If \( MPL <\ APL \), then APL is falling.
  • If \( MPL = APL \), APL is at its maximum.

Diminishing Marginal Returns

As more labor is employed in the short run with capital fixed, the MPL declines due to diminishing marginal returns. Eventually, MPL falls below APL, causing APL itself to decrease.

This relationship guides firms in labor hiring decisions, helping identify the point where workers are used most efficiently.

Long-Run Production Function

In the long run, firms can adjust all inputs, including both labor (L) and capital (K). This flexibility allows firms to respond more effectively to changes in production conditions, overcoming short-run limitations such as the diminishing marginal product of labor.

The long-run production function is given by:

\[ Q = f(K, L) = 15 \cdot K^{0.25} \cdot L^{0.75} \]

  • Q represents total output.
  • K is capital, which is now adjustable in the long run.
  • L is labor.
  • The exponents 0.25 and 0.75 indicate output elasticities, showing the relative contributions of capital and labor.

Adjusting Capital in the Long Run

By adjusting capital to match labor inputs, firms can avoid diminishing marginal returns. For example, hiring more workers without increasing capital may lead to stagnating output. Increasing capital proportionally allows productivity to continue rising.

The marginal product of labor (MPL) in the long run is:

\[ MPL = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial L} = 15 \cdot K^{0.25} \cdot 0.75 \cdot L^{-0.25} \]

As capital (K) increases, the MPL rises, meaning each additional worker contributes more to output because they have more capital to work with.

Substitution Between Capital and Labor

In the long run, firms can also substitute between capital and labor based on their relative costs:

  • If labor becomes more expensive, firms may invest in capital-intensive methods to reduce labor usage.
  • If capital becomes more expensive, firms may shift to labor-intensive production.

This flexibility allows firms to optimize production and maintain efficiency under changing economic conditions.

Production Isoquants

Differences Between Isoquants

Isoquants represent combinations of inputs (Labor L and Capital K) that yield the same level of output. The shape of isoquants depends on the type of production function.

1. Cobb-Douglas Production Function

The Cobb-Douglas production function is:

Q = A · Lα · K1 - α
  • Isoquants are smooth, downward-sloping, and convex to the origin.
  • Labor and Capital are essential and substitutable at a diminishing rate (diminishing marginal rate of technical substitution).
  • Convex shape implies that reducing one input requires increasingly more of the other to maintain the same output.

2. Perfect Substitutes Production Function

The perfect substitutes production function is:

Q = aL + bK
  • Isoquants are straight lines.
  • Labor and Capital can be substituted at a constant rate.
  • The slope is constant, reflecting perfect substitutability between inputs.

3. Perfect Complements (Leontief) Production Function

The perfect complements production function is:

Q = min(L, K)
  • Isoquants are L-shaped.
  • Labor and Capital must be used in fixed proportions to produce output.
  • Excess of one input without increasing the other does not increase output.

Summary

  • Cobb-Douglas: Smooth and convex isoquants, showing diminishing marginal returns and flexible substitution.
  • Perfect Substitutes: Straight-line isoquants, showing constant substitution rates.
  • Perfect Complements: L-shaped isoquants, inputs must be used in fixed proportions.

These shapes highlight the varying relationships between inputs in different production processes.

Returns to Scale

Returns to scale describe how output changes in response to a proportional change in all inputs. For a Cobb-Douglas function, they are determined by α + β:

  • Increasing Returns to Scale: α + β > 1 → doubling inputs produces more than double output. Occurs due to efficiencies like specialization or better capital use.
  • Constant Returns to Scale: α + β = 1 → doubling inputs produces exactly double output. Indicates perfectly scalable production.
  • Decreasing Returns to Scale: α + β < 1 → doubling inputs produces less than double output. Occurs due to inefficiencies or limited resources.

Understanding returns to scale helps firms determine the most efficient scale of production and anticipate output changes when adjusting input levels.

Explanation of Technology Change in Production

Technology change refers to improvements or declines in production efficiency, allowing a firm to produce the same output with fewer or more inputs. In this Cobb-Douglas function, A represents the technology level.

1. Increase in Technology (A > 1)

  • Firms can produce the same output with fewer inputs.
  • Isoquant shifts inward toward the origin.
  • Often results from innovations, better machinery, or efficient processes.

2. Decrease in Technology (A < 1)

  • More inputs are required to produce the same output.
  • Isoquant shifts outward.
  • Could be caused by outdated equipment or inefficiencies.

3. Constant Technology (A = 1)

  • Isoquant remains in the original position.
  • Represents baseline technology without improvements or declines.

Technology is a key determinant of production efficiency. Improvements reduce input costs while maintaining output, enhancing profitability and competitiveness.
