Pricing Strategy

10.1 The Basics of Pricing Strategy

A pricing strategy is a firm’s plan for setting the price of its product based on market conditions and its goal to maximize profit.

  • Perfectly Competitive Firm: Charges the market equilibrium price; cannot influence the price.
  • Firm with Market Power: Sets price where MR = MC.

In this chapter, we focus on price discrimination, where a firm charges different prices to different customers for the same product. This allows a firm with market power to earn greater economic profit than a single-price monopoly.

Key Requirements for Advanced Pricing Strategies

  1. Market Power – The firm can set prices above marginal cost.
  2. Prevention of Resale / Arbitrage – Customers cannot resell the product to others, preventing undercutting of higher prices.

When these conditions are satisfied, firms can employ strategies such as:

  • Direct and indirect price discrimination
  • Bundling multiple products
  • Two-part tariffs

Types of Pricing Strategies

1. Price Discrimination

Requires identifying customers with different price sensitivities of demand.

  • Direct Price Discrimination: The firm knows customer characteristics or demand:
    • First-Degree (Perfect) Price Discrimination: Charges each customer their maximum willingness to pay.
    • Third-Degree Price Discrimination (Segmentation): Charges different prices to distinct groups based on observable traits (e.g., age, location).
  • Indirect Price Discrimination: The firm cannot observe customers’ willingness to pay directly and relies on self-selection:
    • Quantity discounts
    • Multiple product versions at varying prices
    • Coupons

2. Other Pricing Strategies

  • Block Pricing: Different unit prices for different quantities purchased.
  • Two-Part Tariffs: A lump-sum fee plus a per-unit price.
  • Bundling: Selling multiple products together as a package to increase total profit.

Summary

Advanced pricing strategies, especially price discrimination, allow firms with market power to capture more surplus by tailoring prices to customer willingness to pay. The effectiveness depends on:

  • The type and granularity of information the firm has about customers
  • The ability to prevent resale or arbitrage

Strategies like bundling and two-part tariffs extend these principles to extract additional profit from both differentiated and uniform customer groups.

10.2 Direct Price Discrimination I: Perfect (First-Degree)

When to Use It

  • Firm has market power and can prevent resale.
  • Customers have different demand curves.
  • Firm has complete information about each customer.

Concept

Perfect price discrimination occurs when a firm charges each customer their maximum willingness to pay, converting all Consumer Surplus into Producer Surplus. The firm knows each customer’s demand curve and sets prices accordingly.

Requirements for Implementation

  1. Market power to set prices above marginal cost.
  2. Prevention of resale or arbitrage.
  3. Perfect knowledge of each customer’s willingness to pay.

Outcome

  • Consumer Surplus is eliminated.
  • Producer Surplus equals the total area under the demand curve above MC.
  • Deadweight Loss is zero; all consumers willing to pay ≥ MC are served.

Why Segmenting the Market is Better for Producers

Segmenting the market allows producers to increase total producer surplus by charging different prices in different market segments based on consumers’ willingness to pay.

Original Surplus (Without Segmentation)

  • Producer sets a single price for both traveling and local participants.
  • The combined demand curve is flatter due to the larger market, limiting producer surplus.
  • Some potential surplus is lost because a single price cannot capture higher willingness to pay in one segment (e.g., traveling participants).

Surplus After Segmentation

Traveling Market

  • Price charged: PT = 220
  • Quantity: Q = 600
  • Producer surplus: (220 - 100) × 600 = 72,000
  • Surplus represented as the rectangle between P = [100, 220] and Q = [0, 600]

Local Market

  • Price charged: PL = 170
  • Quantity: Q = 700
  • Producer surplus: (170 - 100) × 700 = 49,000
  • Surplus represented as the rectangle between P = [100, 170] and Q = [0, 700]

Total Producer Surplus After Segmentation

Total Producer Surplus = 72,000 + 49,000 = 121,000

Comparison

  • Without segmentation, producer surplus is significantly lower due to a single pricing strategy.
  • Segmentation allows charging higher prices to the traveling market and lower prices to the local market, maximizing total surplus.

Key Takeaways

  • Segmenting the market increases producer surplus by matching prices to the willingness to pay of each market.
  • This strategy captures more potential surplus and avoids the inefficiencies of uniform pricing.

10.3 Direct Price Discrimination II: Segmenting (Third-Degree)

When to Use It:
  • Firm has market power and can prevent resale.
  • Customers have different demand curves.
  • Firm can identify specific groups with different price sensitivities.
Concept: Divide customers into identifiable groups based on characteristics correlated with price sensitivity and charge different prices to each group. Example: Student discounts for school-branded clothing vs. higher prices for parents.

10.4 Indirect (Second-Degree) Price Discrimination

When to Use It:
  • Firm has market power and can prevent resale.
  • Cannot identify customers’ groups before purchase.
Concept: Customers self-select pricing options based on willingness to pay. Example: Airlines offering refundable tickets for business travelers and non-refundable tickets for leisure travelers.

10.5 Bundling

When to Use It:
  • Firm has market power and can prevent resale.
  • Sells multiple products with negatively correlated demand.
Concept: Sell multiple products together at a single price to capture more producer surplus. Example: Streaming services bundling movies and sports packages.

10.6 Advanced Pricing Strategies

When to Use It:
  • Firm has market power and can prevent resale.
  • Customers may have either identical or different demand curves.

Block Pricing

Definition: Reduce the price per unit as customers purchase larger quantities. Works even if customers have identical demand curves.

Example: Single 12-ounce Pepsi can costs $1, six-pack costs $2.99, incentivizing larger purchases.

Two-Part Tariffs

Definition: Pricing has two components: a fixed fee plus a per-unit price.

Examples:
  • Costco membership: $50 annual fee (fixed) + lower per-unit prices on goods.
  • Video game consoles: console price (fixed fee) + individual game prices (per-unit).

Why It Works: Allows the firm to extract consumer surplus via the fixed fee while maintaining efficient consumption levels through the per-unit price.
