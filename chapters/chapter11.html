11.1 What Does Equilibrium Mean in an Oligopoly?

Equilibrium in an oligopoly is more complex than in perfect competition or monopoly because firms’ actions influence each other. Here’s the core idea:

In Simple Markets

  • Equilibrium occurs where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied.
  • No participant wants to change their decision. Example: in a farmer’s market, apple prices adjust to avoid surplus or shortage.

In Oligopolies

  • Firms must consider competitors’ reactions. Example: if one airline lowers prices, others may follow.
  • Equilibrium occurs when each firm chooses its best price or quantity given the strategies of others.
  • No firm can increase profits by changing strategy unilaterally.

Key Point

Oligopoly equilibrium includes both market-clearing (supply = demand) and stability among firms’ strategies. This is known as Nash equilibrium.

Real-World Example

Streaming services like Netflix and Hulu set subscription prices based on competitors. If both settle on prices where neither gains by changing, the market is in equilibrium.

11.2 Oligopoly with Identical Goods: Collusion and Cartels

Key Points

  • Collusion: Firms agree to act like a monopoly by coordinating production and pricing, sharing monopoly profits.
  • Cartel: An organization formed to facilitate collusion among firms.

Why Collusion is Unstable

  1. Market Setup:
    • Inverse demand: P = 20 - Q
    • Marginal cost (MC): $4
  2. Collusive Equilibrium:
    • Total output: Q = 8
    • Price: P = 12
    • Industry profit: (12 - 4) × 8 = 64 dollars
    • Split evenly between Firms A and B: (12 - 4) × 4 = 32 dollars each
  3. Cheating Incentive:
    • If Firm A produces 1 more unit:
    • Total output: Q = 9, lowering price to P = 11
    • Firm A’s profit: (11 - 4) × 5 = 35 dollars
    • This profit incentive makes collusion unstable.

Visual Explanation

The plot below shows the demand curve, marginal revenue curve, and marginal cost:

  • Collusion: At Q = 8, firms collectively maximize profits (P = 12).
  • Cheating: A single firm increases output to Q = 9, reducing the price to P = 11 and gaining a higher profit.

Collusion is unstable because the incentive to cheat disrupts the agreement.

1. Cournot Duopoly: Burton and K2

What Makes Collusion Easier?

  1. Detecting and Punishing Cheaters:
    • Cartels need a way to monitor members and enforce penalties for cheating.
    • Example: In OPEC, countries agree on oil production quotas. If one country exceeds its quota, others may retaliate by increasing production, lowering prices, and reducing the cheater's profits.
    • This enforcement discourages cheating and stabilizes the cartel.
  2. Similar Marginal Costs:
    • When members have similar production costs, agreeing on output levels and prices is easier.
    • Example: A hypothetical dairy farmers' cartel can set a common price per gallon if all farmers have similar milk production costs.
    • Differing costs can lead to disagreements and potential breakdowns.
  3. Long-Term Perspective:
    • Cartels are more stable if members focus on long-term gains rather than short-term profits.
    • Example: Airlines could maintain higher ticket prices by valuing future profits over immediate market share, avoiding price wars that destabilize collusion.

11.3 Bertrand Competition with Identical Goods

Introduction to Bertrand Competition

  • Key Features:
    • Firms sell identical products.
    • Consumers buy from the firm offering the lowest price.
    • If prices are equal, demand is split evenly between firms.
  • Nash Equilibrium:
    • Each firm charges a price equal to its marginal cost.
    • Neither firm can increase its profit by unilaterally changing its price.
    • Firms earn zero economic profit in equilibrium.

Example: Walmart vs. Target

  • Setup:
    • Two stores sell Nintendo Switch consoles with identical marginal costs of $300.
    • Consumers are indifferent between stores aside from price.
    • Demand rule: Buy from the store with the lowest price; split demand evenly if prices are equal.
  • Incentive to Undercut:
    • If Walmart charges $175, Target can undercut with $174.99 and capture the entire market.
    • This triggers continuous undercutting until prices reach marginal cost ($300).
  • Equilibrium:
    • Both firms charge a price equal to marginal cost ($300).
    • Each firm earns zero economic profit and captures half the market.

Insights from Bertrand Competition

  • Even a small number of firms can create intense competition.
  • Outcome is equivalent to perfect competition: Price = Marginal Cost.
  • Examples include online shopping platforms where consumers compare identical products easily.

Why Coordination Fails

  • Firms would benefit from charging prices above marginal cost, but collusion is unstable.
  • The incentive to slightly lower price undermines cooperative pricing strategies.

Generalization

  • Results hold with more than two firms: Each has a strong incentive to undercut competitors.
  • Equilibrium price remains at marginal cost, with firms splitting the market evenly.

11.4 Oligopoly with Identical Goods: Cournot Competition

Model Assumptions

  1. Firms sell identical goods.
  2. Firms compete by choosing production quantities.
  3. Market price is determined by total output from all firms.
  4. Firms choose quantities simultaneously.

Example: Crude Oil Industry

  • Setup:
    • Two producers: Saudi Arabia and Iran.
    • Marginal cost (MC): $20 per barrel.
    • Inverse demand curve: P = 200 - 3Q, where Q = q_S + q_I.
  • Profit Maximization:
    • Saudi Arabia’s marginal revenue: MR_S = 200 - 6q_S - 3q_I.
    • Optimal production: 200 - 6q_S - 3q_I = 20 → q_S = 30 - 0.5q_I.
    • Iran’s reaction curve: q_I = 30 - 0.5q_S.
  • Cournot Equilibrium:
    • Solve reaction curves: q_S = 30 - 0.5(30 - 0.5q_S) → q_S = 20.
    • Substitute into Iran’s curve: q_I = 30 - 0.5(20) = 20.
    • Total output: Q = 40.
    • Price: P = 200 - 3(40) = 80 dollars per barrel.
  • Profits:
    • Each country earns 20 × (80 - 20) = 1,200 million dollars per day.
    • Total industry profit: 2.4 billion dollars per day.

Key Insights

  • Reaction Curves: Show how each firm adjusts production based on the other’s output.
  • Strategic Interaction: Each firm’s decision affects the other’s residual demand.
  • Nash Equilibrium: At q_S = 20, q_I = 20, neither firm can improve profit by changing output.
  • Market Outcome: Price = 80 dollars per barrel. Competition is less intense than Bertrand but more competitive than monopoly.

11.5 Oligopoly with Identical Goods but with a First-Mover: Stackelberg Competition

Model Assumptions

  1. Firms sell identical goods.
  2. Firms compete by choosing quantities.
  3. Market price is determined by total production.
  4. One firm (leader) chooses its quantity first, and the follower reacts.

Example: Saudi Arabia as the Stackelberg Leader

  • Market Setup:
    • Inverse demand: P = 200 - 3Q, where Q = q_S + q_I.
    • Marginal cost (MC): 20 dollars per barrel.
    • Reaction curves: q_S = 30 - 0.5q_I, q_I = 30 - 0.5q_S.
  • Saudi Arabia’s Decision:
    • Substitute Iran’s reaction curve: q_I = 30 - 0.5q_S.
    • Adjusted demand: P = 110 - 1.5q_S.
    • Marginal revenue: MR_S = 110 - 3q_S.
    • Profit maximization: 110 - 3q_S = 20 → q_S = 30.
  • Iran’s Response:
    • Substitute q_S = 30 into reaction curve: q_I = 30 - 0.5(30) = 15.
  • Market Outcomes:
    • Total output: Q = 30 + 15 = 45.
    • Price: P = 200 - 3(45) = 65 dollars per barrel.

Profits

  • Saudi Arabia: 30 × (65 - 20) = 1,350 million dollars/day.
  • Iran: 15 × (65 - 20) = 675 million dollars/day.

Key Takeaways

  • First-Mover Advantage: Saudi Arabia increases output to 30 million barrels/day, forcing Iran to reduce output to 15. Saudi Arabia earns higher profits by moving first.
  • Market Price and Profit: Price drops to 65 dollars/barrel (lower than Cournot price of 80 dollars). Saudi Arabia benefits, while Iran earns less than in Cournot equilibrium.
  • Strategic Implications: The leader uses its position to dictate market conditions, leaving competitors with fewer profitable options.

11.6 Oligopoly with Differentiated Goods: Bertrand Competition

Model Assumptions

  1. Firms sell similar but not identical goods, so consumers do not view them as perfect substitutes.
  2. Firms compete by setting prices.
  3. Firms set prices simultaneously.

Key Insights

  • Differentiated products give firms market power, so price competition is less intense than in the identical-products Bertrand model.
  • Consumers substitute between products imperfectly, limiting extreme undercutting.

Example: Burton vs. K2 (Snowboards)

  • Demand Curves:
    • Burton: q_B = 900 - 2p_B + p_K
    • K2: q_K = 900 - 2p_K + p_B
    • Demand decreases when a firm raises its price and increases when the competitor raises theirs.
  • Revenue and Marginal Revenue:
    • Burton’s total revenue: TR_B = p_B × (900 - 2p_B + p_K)
    • Marginal revenue: MR_B = 900 - 4p_B + p_K
    • K2’s marginal revenue is derived similarly.
  • Reaction Curves:
    • Burton: p_B = 225 + 0.25p_K
    • K2: p_K = 225 + 0.25p_B
    • Reaction curves are upward-sloping: higher competitor prices lead to higher optimal prices, unlike downward-sloping Cournot curves.

Differentiated Bertrand Equilibrium

  • Nash Equilibrium: Occurs where reaction curves intersect:
    • Price: $300 per snowboard
    • Quantity: 600 snowboards per firm
  • Neither firm can increase profit by unilaterally changing its price.
  • Differentiation reduces price competition, allowing positive economic profits.

Graphical Interpretation

  • Reaction curves show how each firm adjusts its price in response to the competitor.
  • Upward-sloping curves illustrate mutual responsiveness: higher competitor prices lead to higher optimal prices.
  • The Nash equilibrium is at the intersection of the curves, representing mutually optimal pricing strategies.

11.7 Monopolistic Competition

Model Assumptions

  1. Firms sell differentiated products that consumers do not view as perfect substitutes.
  2. Firms' residual demand curves are affected by competitors, but strategic interactions are ignored.
  3. There is free entry into the market.

Key Insights

  • Market Dynamics:
    • Firms face downward-sloping demand curves, giving them market power.
    • Positive economic profit attracts new entrants, shifting demand for existing firms.
    • Entry continues until profits are zero, where price equals average total cost (ATC).
  • Characteristics:
    • Despite market power, firms earn zero economic profit in the long run.
    • Differentiation ensures unique demand curves, unlike perfect competition.

Real-World Examples

  • Many fast-food restaurants in a city: slightly different menus compete for the same customers. Entry continues until no firm earns excess profit.

Differences from Oligopoly

  • Entry Barriers: Monopolistic competition has free entry, unlike oligopoly.
  • Strategic Interaction: Oligopolies consider competitors' actions, while monopolistically competitive firms assume competitors’ effects are negligible.

Plot 1: Monopoly (Single Firm)

  • Scenario: A single fast-food restaurant faces demand curve DONE.
  • Equilibrium:
    • Quantity: Q*ONE = 8, where MRONE = MC.
    • Price: P*ONE from the demand curve.
    • Profit: Shaded area between price, ATC, and quantity.
  • Key Insights: ATC includes fixed costs (U-shaped); profit exists because P > ATC.

Plot 2: Two Firms in the Market

  • Scenario: A second restaurant opens with a slightly different product.
  • Impact:
    • Initial demand DONE shifts left and flattens to DTWO.
    • Marginal revenue shifts MRONE → MRTWO.
  • Equilibrium: Each firm produces Q*TWO = 6 at price P*TWO; profits shrink but remain positive.

Plot 3: Long-Run Equilibrium with N Firms

  • Scenario: Entry continues until no firm earns economic profit.
  • Impact:
    • Demand DN flattens and shifts left.
    • Marginal revenue becomes MRN.
    • Quantity: Q*N = 4 at price P*N = ATC*.
  • Equilibrium: Zero profit; entry stops once firms just cover costs.

Key Takeaways

  1. Monopoly: Single firm maximizes profit with steep demand curve and market power.
  2. Short Run (Two Firms): Entry reduces demand for existing firms, lowering price and profits.
  3. Long Run (N Firms): Free entry leads to zero economic profit as demand adjusts to ATC.

Real-World Examples

  • Monopoly: A single neighborhood diner with no nearby competition.
  • Two Firms: Competing fast-food chains in the same area, slightly differentiated menus.
  • Long-Run Equilibrium: Multiple car washes in a city, each just covering costs due to competition.
