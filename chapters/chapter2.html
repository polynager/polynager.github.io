Introduction to Demand

In economics, the demand for a good or service is the quantity that consumers are willing and able to purchase at various prices.

We can represent this relationship between price and quantity demanded using a linear demand function:

$$ Q_d = a - bP $$

Where:

  • $$Q_d$$ is the quantity demanded.
  • $$P$$ is the price.
  • $$a$$ and $$b$$ are constants that determine the intercept and slope of the demand curve.

Factors That Influence Demand

Several factors can influence demand, causing shifts or movements along the demand curve:

  • Movements along the demand curve occur due to changes in the price of the good itself.
  • Shifts of the demand curve occur due to factors like:
    • Changes in consumer preferences.
    • Changes in the price of substitutes or complementary goods.
    • Changes in consumer income or wealth.

Introduction to Supply

The supply curve represents the relationship between the price of a good and the quantity that producers are willing to supply.

We can represent this relationship using a linear supply function:

$$ Q_s = cP - d $$

Where:

  • $Q_s$ is the quantity supplied.
  • $P$ is the price.
  • $c$ and $d$ are constants determining the slope and intercept of the supply curve.

Basic Supply Curve

Factors That Influence Supply

Several factors can cause the supply curve to shift:

  • Movements along the supply curve occur due to changes in the price of the good itself.
  • Shifts of the supply curve occur due to factors such as:
    • Changes in production technology.
    • Changes in the costs of production.
    • Changes in the number of sellers in the market.

Key Concepts Covered:


  
Shifts in Demand:

  
    
    
  • Factors such as changes in consumer preferences, the price of substitutes, and overall market conditions can shift the demand curve.
    • 
    
  • An increase in consumer preferences or a rise in the price of substitutes shifts the demand curve upward, indicating higher quantities demanded at each price level.
    • 
    
  • Conversely, a decrease in consumer preferences or a fall in the price of substitutes shifts the demand curve downward, reducing the quantities demanded at each price level.
    • 
  


  
Shifts in Supply:

  
    
    
  • Advancements in technology, changes in production costs, and market conditions affect the supply curve.
    • 
    
  • Improved technology or lower production costs shift the supply curve downward, reflecting an increase in the quantity supplied at every price level.
    • 
    
  • Higher production costs or reduced technological efficiency shift the supply curve upward, reducing the quantity supplied at each price level.
    • 
  


  
Market Equilibrium:

  
    
    
  • Equilibrium Price and Quantity:  
      The interaction between demand and supply curves determines the market equilibrium—the price at which the 
      quantity demanded equals the quantity supplied.  
      

    
      When demand increases or supply decreases, the equilibrium price typically rises, and the equilibrium quantity may 
      change depending on the extent of the shifts.  
      Conversely, if demand decreases or supply increases, the equilibrium price tends to fall.
    
    • 
    
    
    
  • Market Dynamics:  
      Understanding how both curves interact helps us predict how changes in the market—such as improvements in 
      production technology or shifts in consumer preferences—impact prices and quantities.  
      The size and slope of the demand and supply curves also play a crucial role in determining the magnitude of 
      price and quantity changes.
    
    • 
  


  

    By integrating these principles, we gain insight into how markets adjust and move toward new equilibria, 
    providing a foundation for further analysis of market behavior.
  

  


  

  
Understanding Price Elasticity of Demand


  

    The price elasticity of demand measures how responsive the quantity demanded of a good is to changes in its price. 
    It is defined as:
  


  

    $$
    E_d = \frac{\% \Delta Q_d}{\% \Delta P}
    $$
  


  

    Elasticity provides crucial insights into how consumers react to price changes:
  


  
    
    
  • Elastic Demand: If the elasticity $|E_d| > 1$, demand is considered elastic. 
      Consumers are very responsive to price changes (e.g., luxury goods).
    • 

    
  • Inelastic Demand: If the elasticity $|E_d| < 1$, demand is considered inelastic. 
      Consumers are less responsive to price changes (e.g., necessities like food or gas).
    • 

    
  • Unit Elasticity: If $|E_d| = 1$, the percentage change in quantity demanded equals the percentage change in price.
    • 

    
  • Perfectly Inelastic Demand: If $E_d = 0$, the demand curve is vertical. 
      The quantity demanded does not change with price (e.g., life-saving medications).
    • 

    
  • Perfectly Elastic Demand: If $E_d = \infty$, the demand curve is horizontal. 
      Consumers will only buy at a specific price and no higher.
    •
