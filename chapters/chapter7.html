Sunk Costs and Decision Making

Sunk costs are costs that, once incurred, cannot be recovered, regardless of future decisions. These are distinct from opportunity costs, which should always be considered in decision-making. For example, if you’ve already paid for advertising or signed a non-refundable lease for your restaurant, these expenses are considered sunk costs. Even if the business shuts down, these costs remain, and they do not influence future choices.

When it comes to optimization, sunk costs play no role because they are constant and do not change with respect to the decision variables. Mathematically, the derivative of a constant is zero, which means that sunk costs do not affect the marginal costs or benefits of continuing operations. For example, when a firm is deciding how much to produce, sunk costs should not influence the optimal production level because they are irrelevant to the incremental costs of production.

The key takeaway is that firms (or individuals) should avoid the sunk cost fallacy—the error of letting unrecoverable past expenditures affect future decisions. Instead, decision-making should focus on future costs and revenues, excluding sunk costs from the analysis.

Total Cost, Fixed Cost, and Variable Cost

Costs and Cost Curves

In this chapter, we discuss the economic costs a firm considers when making production decisions. These include both accounting and opportunity costs, accounting for all inputs. A firm’s costs can be divided into two types: fixed costs (FC) and variable costs (VC). Every cost is either fixed or variable, and a firm’s total cost (TC) is the sum of its fixed and variable costs:

Total Cost Equation:

TC(Q) = FC(Q) + VC(Q)

  • Fixed Costs (FC): Costs that remain constant regardless of output, e.g., rent, salaries, long-term leases.
  • Variable Costs (VC): Costs that change with output, e.g., materials and labor that vary with production levels.

Example cost functions:

  • Total Cost (TC): TC(Q) = 5 + 3Q + Q2
  • Fixed Cost (FC): FC(Q) = 5
  • Variable Cost (VC): VC(Q) = 3Q + Q2

Average and Marginal Costs

Average Fixed Cost (AFC): AFC is the fixed cost per unit of output. As output rises, AFC falls because the fixed cost is spread over more units, resulting in a declining curve.

Average Variable Cost (AVC): AVC measures the per-unit variable cost of production. It can rise or fall with output. Initially, AVC often declines due to efficiency gains, but beyond a certain output level, it rises, creating a U-shaped curve.

Average Total Cost (ATC): ATC is total cost per unit of output and equals AFC + AVC. ATC usually exhibits a U-shape because of declining AFC and AVC initially, followed by rising AVC at higher output levels.

Formulas:

  • ATC(Q) = TC(Q) / Q
  • AVC(Q) = VC(Q) / Q
  • AFC(Q) = FC(Q) / Q

In the plot, the Marginal Cost (MC) curve intersects the ATC and AVC curves at their minimum points. If MC is below ATC or AVC, those curves are declining; if MC is above, they are rising.

Marginal Cost (MC): MC is the cost of producing one additional unit:

MC(Q) = dTC(Q)/dQ

The MC curve typically falls at first due to increasing returns, then rises as capacity limits or higher input costs take effect. Its intersection with ATC and AVC explains the U-shape of the average cost curves.
