Chapter 8.1: Market Structures and Perfect Competition in the Short Run

This chapter introduces four types of market structures: perfect competition, monopolistic competition, oligopoly, and monopoly. Market structures are classified based on three main characteristics:

  • Number of Firms: More firms typically increase competition.
  • Product Differentiation: Identical products lead to higher competition; differentiated products reduce it.
  • Barriers to Entry: Low barriers allow easier market entry, increasing competition; high barriers reduce it.

Perfect Competition

A perfectly competitive market has the following features:

  • Number of Firms: Many firms exist, so no single firm can influence the market price.
  • Type of Product: Products are identical, making outputs perfect substitutes.
  • Barriers to Entry: None, allowing free entry and exit.

Key Characteristics

  • Price Takers: Firms can sell as much as they want at the market price but cannot influence it.
  • Market Efficiency: Perfect competition serves as a benchmark where goods are sold at marginal cost, and the sum of producer and consumer surplus is maximized.
  • Firm Demand Curve: For an individual firm, the demand curve is perfectly elastic, represented as a horizontal line at the market price. This shows that the firm has no price-setting power.

This model is rare in real-world markets but provides an important baseline for understanding efficiency and firm behavior in competitive environments.

Chapter 8.2: Profit Maximization in a Perfectly Competitive Market

In a perfectly competitive market, firms maximize profit by selecting the quantity of output where the marginal cost (MC) of production equals the market price (\(P\)), which is also their marginal revenue (MR). Since perfectly competitive firms are price takers, they can only decide how much to produce, not the price at which they sell.

Key Concepts

  • Profit Maximization: A firm maximizes profit by choosing output where \(MC = MR = P\). Producing beyond this point increases costs more than revenue, while producing less reduces potential revenue without lowering costs enough to increase profit.
  • Total Revenue and Total Cost: Profit is the difference between total revenue (\(TR = P \times Q\)) and total cost (\(TC\)).
    • The demand (MR) curve is a horizontal line at the market price.
    • The total revenue curve is a straight line from the origin, as each unit of output adds the same revenue.
  • Profit Calculation: Profit at the optimal output \(Q^*\) can be calculated as: \[ \text{Profit} = (P - ATC) \times Q^* \] where \(ATC\) is the average total cost at \(Q^*\).
  • Output Decisions with Price Changes: If market price changes, the firm adjusts output until \(MC = P\) again. Higher prices lead to higher output, lower prices lead to lower output.

Chapter 8.3: Perfect Competition in the Short Run

Constructing the Short-Run Supply Curve for a Perfectly Competitive Industry

In a perfectly competitive market, each firm’s decision on output does not influence the overall market price individually. However, the combined output of all firms determines the industry supply curve. This aggregation is not a coordinated action by firms but reflects the sum of individual supply decisions. Firms base their output on their own costs and the market price, and summing these quantities across firms produces the total market supply.

The Industry Supply Curve as a Horizontal Sum of Firm-Level Supply Curves

To construct the industry supply curve:

  1. Aggregate individual firm supply curves: For each price level, calculate the output each firm would supply, then sum these quantities across all firms.
  2. Interpretation of supply:
    • If the price is below a firm’s minimum average variable cost (AVC), the firm does not produce.
    • If the price covers costs, the firm supplies a quantity determined by its marginal cost (MC), up to capacity.
Example: A market has 100 identical firms:
  • If \(P < 1\), no firm operates (price does not cover AVC).
  • If \(1 \leq P < 2\), each firm supplies 1 unit → industry supply = \(100 \times 1 = 100\) units.
  • If \(P \geq 2\), each firm supplies 2 units → industry supply = \(100 \times 2 = 200\) units.

Step-by-Step Explanation of the Market Dynamics

Initial Market State

  1. Demand and Supply in the Long Run: \[ Q_{D1} = 30000 - 50P \]

    Long-run supply price:

    \[ P_{LR} = 250 \]

    Industry quantity in initial equilibrium:

    \[ Q_{initial} = Q_{D1}(P=250) = 30000 - 50 \cdot 250 = 17500 \]
  2. Firm-Level Details: \[ TC = 300Q - 10Q^2 + 0.5Q^3 \] \[ MC = 300 - 20Q + 1.5Q^2 \] \[ ATC = 300 - 10Q + 0.5Q^2 \]

    Long-run firm output:

    \[ Q_{firm} = 10 \]
  3. Equilibrium Point:

    At \(P = 250\), industry quantity = 17500, firm quantity = 10. This establishes the initial long-run equilibrium.

Effect of Demand Shift on Market Equilibrium

  1. Demand Shift: \[ Q_{D2} = (30000 + \text{demand shift}) - 50P \]

    New quantity demanded at \(P = 250\):

    \[ Q_{new} = Q_{D2}(P=250) \]
  2. New Short-Run Equilibrium:

    Price rises to meet increased demand. The new intersection of demand with short-run supply occurs at a higher price.

Firm-Level Adjustment in the Short Run

  1. New Quantity per Firm: \[ Q_{firm}^{SR} = \frac{Q_{new}}{\text{initial number of firms}} \]
  2. Short-Run Production Price: \[ P_{new} = MC(Q_{firm}^{SR}) \]

    This price and quantity mark the firm-level adjustment in the short run.

Short-Run Market Adjustment with Temporary Supply Curve

  1. Short-Run Supply Curve:

    The slope of the short-run supply curve:

    \[ m = \frac{P_2 - P_1}{Q_2 - Q_1} \]

    Extended line shows the short-run supply curve.

Long-Run Return to Equilibrium

  1. Entry of New Firms:

    Higher prices attract new firms, increasing industry supply and lowering the price back toward \(P_{LR} = 250\).

  2. New Long-Run Quantity:

    The market reaches a new long-run equilibrium with the same price but higher industry quantity.
